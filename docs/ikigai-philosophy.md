# The Philosophy Behind the Questions

This document explains the conceptual and psychological foundations of the Ikigai test — why the questions were designed the way they were, and what each is trying to surface.

---

## What Ikigai Actually Is

The word *ikigai* (生き甲斐) combines *iki* (生き, "life") and *gai* (甲斐, "worth, value, benefit"). It translates loosely as "that which makes life worth living" — a reason to get up in the morning.

The four-circle Venn diagram commonly associated with Ikigai in the West was popularised by entrepreneur Marc Winn in 2014, who overlaid a Western model of *purpose* (from Andrés Zuzunaga) onto a Japanese concept that originally had a more intimate, everyday meaning. In Japan, ikigai can be as simple as a morning cup of tea, a grandchild's laughter, or the anticipation of a hobby.

The four-circle model, while not purely Japanese, is a genuinely useful framework for Western audiences navigating questions of vocation and meaning. This test uses it as a **reflective instrument**, not a diagnostic one.

---

## The Four Dimensions

### 1. Passion — What You Love (愛すること)
**What we're measuring:** Intrinsic motivation, flow states, and activities pursued for their own sake — not for reward or recognition.

**Why indirect questions work better:** Direct questions ("what do you love?") activate social desirability bias — people list "reading", "spending time with family", or socially approved answers. Indirect scenario-based questions (what do you do on a free afternoon? what would you read with no one watching?) bypass this and reach genuine, habitual behaviour.

**Psychological grounding:**
- Csikszentmihalyi's **flow theory** — activities where challenge meets skill, time disappears, and engagement is effortless
- Deci & Ryan's **intrinsic motivation** (Self-Determination Theory) — activities done for inherent satisfaction
- Seligman's **engagement** dimension in PERMA

---

### 2. Vocation / Mastery — What You're Good At (得意なこと)
**What we're measuring:** Self-perceived competence, accumulated skill, and how others experience your contributions.

**Design note:** We avoid asking "what are your strengths?" directly because most people either over-claim (listing aspirational traits) or under-claim (imposter syndrome). Instead, we ask about *roles in groups*, *what others come to you for*, and *the thread running through your best work* — all more behaviourally grounded.

**Psychological grounding:**
- Peterson & Seligman's **VIA Character Strengths** classification
- Gladwell-adjacent research on deliberate practice and domain expertise
- **Reflected best-self** methodology — what do people tell you you're good at when they're being honest?

---

### 3. Profession — What You Can Be Paid For (稼げること)
**What we're measuring:** Comfort with economic self-advocacy, belief in the market value of one's abilities, and the gap (or alignment) between what one does and what sustains one financially.

**Design note:** This is the dimension most people in meaning-seeking contexts are ambivalent about. Many feel it's crass to think about money alongside passion. Questions in this dimension are designed to probe that ambivalence honestly — e.g. the question about being offered payment for your favourite thing deliberately surfaces whether the person sees their gifts as monetisable.

**Psychological grounding:**
- Research on **financial self-efficacy** and economic confidence
- The **hobby-to-vocation transition** literature (concerns about "ruining" something loved by making it a job)
- Concepts from **positive career psychology** (Savickas, Lent)

---

### 4. Mission — What the World Needs (世界が必要とすること)
**What we're measuring:** Prosocial orientation, sense of social responsibility, and connection to something larger than personal success.

**Design note:** This is the hardest dimension to measure honestly because "caring about the world" is almost universally claimed. Questions avoid virtue-signalling territory and instead ask about *which* problems keep you up at night, and *what role* you envision your work playing — these reveal where concern is genuine versus performed.

**Psychological grounding:**
- Frankl's **logotherapy** and the centrality of meaning beyond the self
- Research on **prosocial motivation** and its relationship to sustained engagement
- **Self-transcendence** as the apex of Maslow's revised hierarchy

## Question Design Principles

### 1. No leading questions
Every question is designed so that no single answer is obviously "more Ikigai-aligned" than another. There is no right answer. This reduces the test-taking effect (trying to score well) and improves ecological validity.

### 2. Scenario and projection-based formats
Questions use real or imagined scenarios ("on a free afternoon...", "if you could witness one shift in the world...") because scenario-based questions are better predictors of actual behaviour and values than attitude questions.

### 3. Shuffled dimensionality
Questions from the four dimensions are deliberately interleaved so that users cannot detect which area is being measured. This further reduces strategic answering.

### 4. Varied formats for cognitive variety
Using three question types (multiple choice, Likert, emoji selection) maintains engagement and also captures different response modes — analytical, gradated, and associative/intuitive.

### 5. No trick questions or negative scoring
The test does not try to catch inconsistencies or penalise answers. It assumes good faith and rewards honest reflection.

## What the Results Are — and Aren't

The results of this test are **reflective portraits**, not diagnoses. A low score in one dimension does not mean you lack that quality — it may mean:
- You haven't had the opportunity to develop or express it
- The questions didn't resonate with how you experience that dimension
- You're at a transition point where old answers no longer apply

The most useful frame for the results is **"an invitation to inquiry"** — a starting point for journaling, conversation, or deeper exploration, not a label.

## Further Reading

- **Ken Mogi** — *The Little Book of Ikigai* (2017): The most psychologically grounded popular treatment
- **Héctor García & Francesc Miralles** — *Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life* (2016): The book that popularised the concept in the West
- **Michiko Kumano** — Research on *ikigai-kan* (the feeling of ikigai) as a measurable psychological construct
- **Tadanori Imai et al.** — *Ikigai-kan Scale* (2012): The most validated psychometric instrument in the literature
- **Viktor Frankl** — *Man's Search for Meaning* (1946): The closest Western parallel to ikigai philosophy
- **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi** — *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience* (1990): Essential for understanding the "love" dimension
